History

Wikipedia says about go,

"Go is an abstract strategy board game for two players in which the aim is to surround more territory than the opponent. The game was invented in China more than 2,500 years ago and is believed to be the oldest board game continuously played to the present day. A 2016 survey by the International Go Federation's 75 member nations found that there are over 46 million people worldwide who know how to play Go and over 20 million current players, the majority of whom live in East Asia."

How to Play

Basics

The rules of go are fairly straightforward. Each player plays stones of one color, black or white. Generally, the less experienced player plays the black stones and takes the first turn. A turn consists of placing a stone of your color on any intersection of lines on the board. At any point a player may choose to pass their turn. When both players pass the game is considered finished and can be scored.

Life and Death

When the first stone is played in the middle of the board (or anywhere that is not on the outer edge of the board) it is considered to have four(4) liberties. Liberties are what determine if a stone or group of stones is/are considered dead or alive.


When two stones of the same color are directly adjacent to each other, they are considered to be connected and therefore, share their liberties. Stones are not considered connected if the are diagonally adjacent only.


Stone of opposing colors do not share liberties. If a black group completely surrounds a white group, and the white group has no internal liberties (called "eyes"), black captures the white group and keeps them to add to black's score at the end of the game.



You can capture large groups as well as individual stones.
You are not allowed to play moves that would be "suicidal" or moves that would cause one of your own group's liberties to be reduced to zero. However, a move can create liberties by capturing, allowing you to play in spaces that would otherwise be suicidal.



The only way to guarantee that a group will not be captured is to give it (at least) two eyes, or liberties the are surrounded by the group. As we saw in a previous example, one eye is not enough because, if the external liberties are all covered by the opponent, our single eye can be filled. But the opponent can only play one stone per turn. Therefore, if the group has two internal liberties (eyes) it can never be captured. More eyes are better(more points!), but if you have two, you will never die.



Ko

Ko can be a very tricky concept. If it were not for the rule of ko, it would be easy for games to repeat the same two board positions over and over again as the players fight for a single capture with no way to resolve it. The website sensei's library does a good job of explaining this rule.

Score

There are two main sets of rules for scoring a game of go, area scoring and territory scoring. In territory scoring, your score is the number of empty points surrounded by only your stones plus the number of stones you captured from your opponent. If you played white, you also get extra points, called komi, for going second. Usually komi is worth 6.5 points. This is to compensate for going second. It also means that the scores can not be tied because black can not score half of a point.

Where to play

The most popular website for English speakers to play go on is OGS (Online Go Server). It is also the easiest to use because it only requires an account, which is free to sign up for. You can play in your browser, so no software needs to be downloaded or installed, which is very convenient.

There are other servers such as Fox and Tygem which are more popular among players in Eastern Asia and more competitive players. However, both of these servers require the user to download and install their software and, due to their age, are generally less user-friendly than OGS.
